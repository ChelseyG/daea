Gebelein

Pre-Dynastic to Middle Kingdom

Introduction

During the Napoleonic expeditions to Egypt in the early nineteenth century, preliminary recordings of sites were noted in the now-famous manuscript, D’escription de L’Egypte. One of these sites was Gebelein, the remains of a settlement from the pre-dynastic era all the way to the Middle Kingdom. Along with other sites such as Abydos and Hierakonpolis, Gebelein featured a rich variety of archaeological finds, from mummies to a pleated dress. Like other sites discovered and excavated during the nineteenth century, artifacts and human remains brought to Europe and exhibited in museums educated the public, and brought awareness to Egyptology.


Location and Geographical Setting

Gebelein, also known as Gebelên, is located approximately 32 km (~20 miles) south of Thebes in Upper Egypt, along the western side of the Nile River, in a place currently known as Naga el-Gherira (Oxford Reference) (Bard, 402). The name Gebelein is Arabic for “the two mountains”, and was influenced by the two hills surrounding the site – Gebelein is located on the slopes of the southern hill and the plain of the northern hill (Bard, 402) . The northern hill is the location of a cemetery, while the southern hill is the location of the Temple of Hathor.

Similar to other settlements at the time, Gebelein’s close location to the Nile River provided the necessary resources for the settlement to function. In addition, Gebelein also contains geological minerals that have demonstrated use in Gebelein's past. One such example is limestone, which was used to carve stela found in and around the Temple of Hathor. Another example is the amount of sand around Gebelein, which allowed the human remains buried on the northern hill to dry out instead of decomposing.

Excavations

Although Gebelein was mentioned in D’escription de L’Egypte in the early 1800s, first excavations began near the end of the nineteenth century. Wallis Budge, an Egyptologist for the British Museum, stated in By Nile and Tigris, “In the greater number of tombs from which [pre-dynastic antiquities] came, the human remains were much broken, and generally speaking, not worth removing”. (Budge, 359). Thus, early expeditions resulted in the valuable finds that encouraged further excavation in hopes of adding to the growing Ancient Egyptian collection at the British Museum.

Early excavators of Gebelein included the French, with Gaston Maspero starting excavations in 1884. His investigations were followed by his fellow Frenchmen, Eugène Grébaut and Georges Daressy in 1891. And subsequently Jacques de Morgan and Georges Foucart in 1893. Jacques de Morgan returned for more investigations in 1900. Eight years later, Louis Lortet and Claude Gaillard investigated the site. The Egyptian Exploration Fund also sent G. W. Fraser and M. W. Blackden in 1893 to excavate Gebelein. In the early twentieth century, the Egyptian Musuem in Turin, Italy send Ernesto Schiaparellis, who would conduct a series of excavations from 1910 to 1920, followed by his successor, Giulio Farina from 1930 to 1937 (Bard, 403).

The cemeteries located on the northern hill contained mummies and grave goods from the pre-dynastic era, particularly the fourth millennium BC. Early excavations of these cemeteries were focused on finding artifacts and human remains that could potentially be used for exhibition in the British Museum. Preliminary searches yielded only fragmented remains, however, further investigation due to information from a Gebelein native resulted in the discovery of complete sets of human remains. These remains were buried in shallow graves with assorted grave goods such as pots and flints (Budge, 360). One of the most famous of these remains is a naturally mummified male – dubbed “Ginger” due to his red hair – who remains at the British Museum today. The mummified male was found buried in a contracted, fetal-like position – similar to other contemporary burials and covered directly with earth (most likely sand), resulting in the natural mummification (The British Museum). Although the mummified male was discovered over a century ago, research is still being conducted on the mummy. In particular, an analytical request regarding the light levels in the showcase was proposed, as it was believed that the level of light could damage the mummy. Results influenced not only the lowering of light in the mummy’s showcase, but for the whole gallery, in order to protect and preserve the artifacts (The British Museum) Gebelein is also the source of artifacts from the later periods of Ancient Egyptian history. Aquisitioned in 1931, a round-topped limestone stela depicts Wedjat eyes, two rows of hieroglyphic text, and a depiction of a deceased holding a lotus flower (The British Museum). The hieroglyphic inscription translates to “Guard of the Phyle of Hathor” and “Lady of Gebelein” (The British Museum). This stela was dated to be a remnant of the Second Intermediate or Middle Kingdom era – approximately 1500 – 2000 years later than the pre-dynastic mummies discovered in the northern hill necropolis. The stela is just one of the many artifacts associated with the dynastic era of Ancient Egypt in Gebelein. During the early twentieth century, Ernesto Schiaparelli led a group of Italian archaeologists in the excavation of Gebelein, and in particular, the Temple of Hathor. Bard described the temple as, “located on the southern hill close to the Nile, where a manmade cave was excavated in a ‘T’ plan, consisting of a hall and a sanctuary (Bard, 402). Other artifacts found at the temple include royal stelae from the second and third dynasties (early dynastic and old kingdom), wall fragments depicting royalty from the eleventh, thirteenth, and fifteenth dynasties (Middle Kingdom and Second Intermediate Period), and a wall of mud bricks form the late pharaonic era (Oxford Reference) (Bard, 402). Furthermore, Gebelein proved to be a sight with artistic significance. Various figurines including one of a bearded man, and wooden models of boats were discovered and dated to the pre-dynastic era and the First Intermediate Period, respectively (Bard, 403). Furthermore, a group of sarcophogai dating to the First Intermediate Period was also found. These sarcophogai featured illustrations and painting of scenes (Bard, 403). Examples of literature and education are exemplified in papyri consisting of administrative texts found in Fourth Dynasty graves (Bard, 404). These finds illustrate the significance of Gebelein over time, and not during one specific period. In addition, the hieroglyphics on the stelae and the papyri and creation of the temple can give insight into the education and technology of the Gebelein community during the dynastic era.

Lastly, Schiaparelli also discovered a sixth-dynasty tomb consisting of a linen dress in the necropolis on the northern hill. The “Turin dress” was discovered along with other grave objects such as a wooden head-rest, a stone vase, and a decayed pair of leather sandals. Unfortunately, due to lost records, the location of the latter three objects is currently unknown (Hall and Pedrini, 136). Hall and Pedrini discuss the dress in great detail in their article, explaining how the dress is made up of three separate parts and held together by strings. Furthermore, inferences are made in regards to the use of the dress – a lack of pleating and appearance of possible perspiration suggest that the garment was worn during the individual’s lifetime and not just used for funerary purposes (Hall and Pedrini, 138). Fiber analysis of the dress was also taken, and the results concluded that materials used in the dress included bast fibers and flax or hemp (Hall and Pedrini, 138). Furthermore, the Turin dress was compared to dresses found in a First Intermediate Period cemetery at Naga ed-Dêr. Although the Turin dress is only one artifact and much of its context is unknown, analysis of the design and materials concluded that the dress was most likely worn by a woman, but the dress was also an anomaly, possibly caused by differences in the regional climate between Gebelein in central Upper Egypt and the location of the other dresses in the Fayum and northern Upper Egypt.

Although excavated materials such as the linen dress and the mummified male remains with ginger-colored hair are still articles of inquiry in recent scholarship, as of late, little excavation has been done at Gebelein. However, the site on the northern hill where the cemetery is located has only been partially investigated, and this necropolis has the potential for future discoveries should excavations at Gebelein be resumed. Research has demonstrated the significance of Gebelein from the pre-dynastic period to at least the Middle Kingdom era, exhibited through the discoveries of pre-dynastic mummies and the Temple of Hathor. Nevertheless, archaeological data collecting is still going on in Gebelein. As of 1999, attempts to accurately map Gebelein were taking place (Bard, 403). Thus, recent scholarship of Gebelein has focused on the analysis of already-excavated materials opposed to the propositions of new excavations at the site.


Conclusion

As one of the original sites discovered during the Napoleonic expeditions, and the location of many excavations over the past century, Gebelein not only represents Ancient Egyptian history, but also the history of archaeological discovery. Originally seen as a potential source for antiquities intended to be put on display at the British Museum, Gebelein now also represents the influence Europe – in particular to Gebelein: France, England, and Italy – had upon the discovery of Ancient Egyptian artifacts. These discoveries led to public awareness, which ultimately resulted in not only the collecting of Ancient Egyptian artifacts, but also the study of Ancient Egyptian life and culture.
